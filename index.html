Development

Process

This section explains how the work develops from rough ideas into detailed drawings. Universities often value seeing how you think, not only the final image.

How I Develop a Piece

1

Concept

I begin with a symbol, object, creature, or strange image that could suggest a story.

2

Composition

I test scale, balance, and placement so the drawing feels dramatic and readable.

3

Texture

I build tone using repeated lines, hatching, cross-hatching, and dense mark-making.

4

Final Details

I add small symbols, shadows, borders, and focal points to make the piece feel complete.

I am interested in how small details can make a drawing feel like part of a larger story. This portfolio shows my development in pencil, ink, composition, and visual storytelling.

Featured

Skull Passage

Ink and pencil surreal composition with layered worlds, symbols, and architectural forms.

About the Artwork

Skull Passage is a surreal drawing built around a large skull that functions as both the central subject and an entrance into another world. The skull dominates the page, while the smaller landscape inside and beneath it suggests that an entire environment exists within the figure. This difference in scale encourages the viewer to move between the overall image and its miniature details.

The staircase at the top creates a path toward an unknown doorway, while the circular forms, arches, trees, figures, and lower pointed landscape make the composition feel like a connected series of spaces. Ink, pencil, hatching, and cross-hatching were used to separate light and dark areas and create a mysterious atmosphere.

Medium: Ink and pencil

Main ideas: Hidden worlds, passageways, scale, mystery, and mortality

Techniques: Hatching, cross-hatching, miniature detail, and layered composition

Sketchbook

Jellyfish Study

Graphite study focused on organic movement, transparent form, and soft value changes.

About the Artwork

Jellyfish Study examines the structure and movement of a jellyfish through graphite. The rounded bell provides the main mass of the drawing, while the long tentacles and layered central forms create a downward sense of motion. The composition leaves open space around the subject so the delicate silhouette remains easy to read.

Soft shading was used to suggest transparency and volume without giving the form a hard, solid appearance. Darker values beneath the bell and within the central body help create depth, while the lighter outer edges preserve the jellyfish's fragile and floating quality.

Medium: Graphite

Main ideas: Organic movement, transparency, delicacy, and underwater life

Techniques: Gradual shading, contour drawing, value control, and flowing linework

Serpent Eclipse

Graphite and ink drawing using contrast, scale, and symbolism.

About the Artwork

Serpent Eclipse combines a skull, a coiled serpent, surrounding rock formations, and a darkened celestial body. The serpent wraps around the skull and rises toward the eclipse, connecting the earthbound subject to the sky. This creates a circular movement that repeatedly guides the viewer through the composition.

Celestial Eye

Detailed ink study exploring symmetry, line weight, and radiating texture.

About the Artwork

Celestial Eye is an abstract, symmetrical design that combines the visual language of an eye, a star, and a celestial symbol. Featured

Eye in die

Textural ink drawing using repeated marks, symbols, dense architectural shapes, all while only using the word "die".

About the Artwork

Eye in die uses the repeated word “die” as both written language and drawing material. From a distance, the repeated marks form a giant eye, towers, arches, and a crowded environment. At a closer distance, the viewer can recognize that the textures and outlines are built from the same word over and over again.

The artwork explores how repetition can change the meaning of a word. Instead of acting only as text, the word becomes shadow, architecture, pattern, and visual noise. The eye watches over the lower structures, creating a tense atmosphere and making the composition feel like an imagined city formed from one obsessive thought.

Medium: Ink

Main ideas: Repetition, language, observation, anxiety, architecture, and hidden meaning

Techniques: Typographic drawing, dense mark-making, pattern, contrast, and large-scale composition

Color Work

Skull Space

Mixed graphite and colored pencil piece combining a skull portrait with a space scene.

About the Artwork

Skull Space combines a close-up skull with a small space scene contained inside the eye socket. The large skull creates an immediate and familiar symbol of mortality, while the spacecraft, Earth, and burst of warm light introduce a much larger cosmic setting within a small part of the image.

This contrast in scale makes the eye socket function like a window or portal. Most of the skull remains monochrome, while the coloured space scene attracts attention through its blue, orange, and yellow tones. The difference between graphite and coloured pencil separates the physical skull from the imagined view beyond it.

Medium: Graphite and coloured pencil

Main ideas: Space, mortality, portals, scale, exploration, and imagination

Techniques: Selective colour, value contrast, close cropping, and nested imagery

Color Work

Black Hole View

Colored pencil space composition focused on scale, atmosphere, and contrast.

About the Artwork

Black Hole View places a small spacecraft between Earth and a massive black hole. The size difference between these subjects emphasizes the enormous scale of space and the vulnerability of the spacecraft. Earth enters from the lower corner, while the black hole fills most of the upper composition.

Warm yellow and orange rings surround the dark centre, creating an intense contrast between light and emptiness. The limited colour palette and open space help create isolation, while the small spacecraft provides a point that viewers can use to understand the scale of the scene.

Medium: Coloured pencil

Main ideas: Cosmic scale, exploration, isolation, danger, and the unknown

Techniques: Colour gradients, dramatic scale, negative space, and focal contrast

The Smoking Guy

Ink illustration with detailed borders, miniature symbols, and narrative humor.

About the Artwork

The Smoking Guy presents a skeleton inside a tomb-like frame, surrounded by a large collection of miniature objects and symbols. The central skeleton remains simple and easy to recognize, while the detailed border encourages the viewer to spend more time searching through the smaller visual information.

The title and smoking skeleton introduce dark humour into a subject associated with death. A skull and sword sit above the main figure, while the objects around the frame suggest memories, possessions, habits, or fragments of a life. The dense pile of marks beneath the tomb adds visual weight and makes the central space feel enclosed.

Medium: Ink

Main ideas: Dark humour, mortality, memory, habits, objects, and storytelling

Techniques: Miniature detail, symbolic borders, repetition, line drawing, and visual contrast
